FROM New York to California, outrage over the acquittal of George Zimmerman in the shooting death of an unarmed black teenager has poured from street demonstrations and church pulpits. 
Protesters called for justice for the victim and demanded federal civil rights charges against the neighbourhood watch volunteer who killed him.
The Florida case has unleashed a national debate over racial profiling, self-defence and equal justice.
In Washington, the Justice Department said it was looking into the case to determine whether federal prosecutors should file criminal civil rights charges now that Mr Zimmerman had been acquitted in the state case.
In a statement on Sunday, the Justice Department said the criminal section of its civil rights division, the FBI and the US Attorney's office for the Middle District of Florida were evaluating evidence.
President Barack Obama called the death of 17-year-old Trayvon Martin a tragedy for the country and urged calm reflection, a message shared by religious and civil rights leaders hoping to ensure peaceful demonstrations in the wake of a case that became an emotional flash point.
In Manhattan, congregants at Middle Collegiate Church were encouraged to wear hooded sweatshirts in memory of Martin, who was wearing a hoodie
the night he was shot to death in
February 2012.
The Reverend Jacqueline Lewis, wearing a pink hoodie, urged peace and told her congregation that slain civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr ``would have wanted us to conduct ourselves on the highest plane of dignity''.
